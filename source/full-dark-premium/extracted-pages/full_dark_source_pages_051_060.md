PART III 
INFERNO AND ISOLATION 
CHAPTER 7 
ADRIFT IN NORTH DAKOTA 
After Garrett died, the unraveling didn’t happen fast. 
It stretched. 
Frayed. 
Like a rope taking weight it was never meant to carry. 
By the time my marriage finally collapsed in 2020, the fire had already been burning for years. 
The opiate addiction that started when I was seventeen had changed shape around 2015. 
Pills gave way to Suboxone for six months. 
Just long enough to convince me I was stabilizing. 
Then meth took over. 
Sometimes injected. 
Sometimes traded for alcohol. 
One master replacing another. 
The illusion of choice hiding the same captivity. 
When Trinity took the kids, the house emptied out in a way that felt physical. 
Four bedrooms reduced to echoes. 
I couldn’t afford it alone. 
Didn’t need the space anyway.

I slept on a buddy’s floor for a while, my life compressed down to whatever could fit beside a 
mattress and a pipe. 
I needed distance. 
A reset. 
Somewhere far enough away that the noise might finally stop. 
So I went north. 
Williston, North Dakota. 
Full-time work at Love’s Truck Stop. 
Cold air. 
Long hours. 
Structure. 
I told myself it was a fresh start. 
What I didn’t understand yet was that you can’t outrun a nervous system. 
You just give it new terrain. 
The fire came with me. 
Work became the next addiction. 
It was cleaner. 
Legal. 
Praised. 
I threw myself into it with the same compulsive intensity I had always used to survive. 
If I stayed busy, maybe I wouldn’t feel the weight of my mother’s death in December 2021. 
If I stayed moving, maybe the divorce wouldn’t catch me. 
If I stayed useful, maybe the ache of my kids being gone wouldn’t swallow me whole. 
Stocking. 
Cleaning.
